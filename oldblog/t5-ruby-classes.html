The one with the Classes


10/17/2014 :: Phase 0 Week 5

This week has been chock-full of creating classes, methods, instance variables, instance methods, and all that jazz. This week made me remember a TA from college. She was TAing an English/Humanities, but she used to be a nurse in the neurological department. She would always say:

Anytime you are working on something, whether it is thinking through logic/philosophy, or trying to solve something, there will be a time when you run into a wall. A wall your brain feels. This is usually when most people will give up, but really it's just the brain trying to create new neurological connections. Don't give up, keep chipping at that wall and it will follow through for you.

That being said, you should always learn that at some certain extent you need to stop to at least take a break; because next thing you know you just realized you've been chipping at the wrong thing the whole time... Now onto the topic that gave me such a blockage this past week:

"Know-How"


 class FriendsThanksgiving
      attr_accessor :dish
      def initialize(new_dish)   <-- instance method 
        @dish = new_dish   <-- @dish is an instance variable 
        puts "Let's have #{@dish} for our Friends' Thanksgiving!"
      end
      def prepare_instead(new_dish)   <-- instance method 
        @dish = new_dish
        puts "We now have #{@dish} for the Friends' Thanksgiving."
      end
      def locked_out(new_dish) <-- instance method 
        @dish.insert(0,"burnt ")
        put "Got locked out; Friends now have burnt #{@dish} for Thanksgiving..."
      end
  end

  monica = FriendsThanksgiving.new("potatoes with lumps")
  => Let's have potatoes with lumps for our Friends' Thanksgiving!
  monica.dish
  => "potatoes with lumps"
    # Ross wanted lumps but now Joey wants his Thanksgiving with potatoes
  a different way:
  monica.prepare_instead("potatoes in the form of tots")
  => We now have potatoes in the form of tots for the Friends' Thanksgiving.
  monica.dish
  => "potatoes in the form of tots" 
    # Oh, but wait Phoebe's mom used to make Thanksgiving potatoes another way: 
  monica.prepare_instead("potatoes with peas and onions")
  => We now have potatoes with peas and onions for the Friends' Thanksgiving.
  monica.dish
  => "potatoes with peas and onions"

Alright, here's the first example. We have:

  • class: a template created for the instance FriendsThanksgiving. (Each year they all have different shenanigans.)
  • instance variable: a defined variable that will be used within the instance methods of a class. In this instance, I focus on potatoes and the specific type Monica will cook for the instance FriendsThanksgiving.
  • instance method: I focused on the instance when types of potatoes were in question, but I want to change it from my originally defined type. The instance method takes in a parameter to actively change it into something else (say, in the form of tots or with peas and onions).

Now we have another instance for another year(instance) and another interesting dish(instance variable):

  rachel = FriendsThanksgiving.new("English Trifle")
  => Let's have English Trifle for our Friends' Thanksgiving!
  rachel.prepare_instead("Shepherd's Pie")
  => We now have Shepherd's Pie for the Friends' Thanksgiving.
  # Ultimately, what Rachel actually makes...
  rachel.prepare_instead("half an english trifle and half a shepherd's Pie")
  => We now have half an english trifle and half a shepherd's Pie for the Friends'
  Thanksgiving.
  # But of course with Friends, sometimes things just go wrong:
  monica.locked_out
  => Got locked out; Friends now have burnt potatoes with peas and onions for
  Thanksgiving...
  rachel.locked_out
  => Got locked out; Friends now have burnt half an english trifle and half a
  shepherd's Pie for Thanksgiving...
